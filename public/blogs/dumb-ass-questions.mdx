<C>
Dumb questions exist. And they’re not just annoying, they’re a sign that something is fundamentally broken. Let me define it for you: a dumb question is any question that could’ve been answered by a little bit of effort on your part. If the answer exists out there, easily accessible in a documentation page, a Google search, or a previously shared resource, then congratulations, you’ve just asked a dumb fucking question.
</C>

<C>
Now, before you get defensive, let me clarify: the problem isn’t just you. Dumb questions are a symptom of **inefficient systems, poor documentation, and lazy habits.** If you have to ask something obvious, it means there’s a gap in the system. And gaps are the enemy of productivity and progress.
</C>

<C>
A non-dumb question, on the other hand, is something you’ve **worked to figure out but couldn’t.** You’ve searched, you’ve read, you’ve tried, and you’ve come up short. Maybe the documentation is unclear, maybe the information doesn’t exist in any accessible form, or maybe it’s highly specialized knowledge that only a specific person can provide. That’s when it’s worth asking someone, because you’re not wasting their time, and you’re respecting their expertise.
</C>

<C>
This is why systems matter. In software, for example, we emphasize documentation because it’s the antidote to dumb questions. Every critical decision, every architectural choice, every key implementation detail should be documented. Why? So people don’t waste time repeating the same conversations, rediscovering the same solutions, or asking the same dumb questions.
</C>

<C>
The same principle applies to education. The traditional educational system is a disaster because it’s built on inefficiency. Professors stand in front of classrooms, repeating the same lectures year after year to different groups of 90 students. Why? That information could be recorded once, turned into a scalable, repeatable resource, and distributed to millions. Instead of repeating themselves endlessly, educators could focus on **coaching** the students who truly need help, those with unique, unanswered questions.
</C>

<C>
If you’re asking dumb questions, it’s often because the system around you is broken. And if you’re hearing dumb questions all day long, it’s time to fix that system. Preserve the knowledge. Document everything. Share it widely. Build mechanisms for distributing information efficiently so no one has to ask the same thing twice.
</C>

<C>
This isn’t just a productivity hack, it’s the essence of progress. Think about it: human civilization only advanced because we learned how to preserve knowledge. Writing was invented so that ideas and discoveries wouldn’t be lost with the death of the person who came up with them. If knowledge isn’t preserved, it gets forgotten. If it gets forgotten, humanity repeats its mistakes.
</C>

<C>
You’ve probably heard theories about ancient civilizations that lost all their advancements because they failed to preserve their knowledge. But the lesson remains the same: **knowledge lost is humanity stalled.** The same applies to your team, your company, your project. If information isn’t documented and distributed, you’re setting yourself up for failure.
</C>

<C>
In software, repeating tasks is a cardinal sin. You don’t rewrite the same function ten times, you abstract it. You don’t manually deploy your app every time, you automate it. The same mindset applies to knowledge. If you’re repeating the same explanations or answering the same questions over and over, you’re doing it wrong. The solution is simple: create systems that preserve and distribute knowledge so effectively that dumb questions disappear.
</C>

<C>
A dumb question isn’t just a waste of time, it’s a reflection of inefficiency. If you’re in an environment where dumb questions are the norm, fix the system. Build better documentation. Create better resources. Share knowledge widely and make it digestible. The fewer dumb questions people ask, the closer you are to having a truly efficient, productive team.
</C>

<C>
Because at the end of the day, a dumb question isn’t just a sign of laziness, it’s a symptom of a broken process. Fix the process, and you’ll fix the questions.
</C>
